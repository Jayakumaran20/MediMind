Hypertension (High Blood Pressure) — Clinical Overview

Definition
Hypertension is a chronic condition where the force of blood against the artery
walls is persistently elevated. Blood pressure is measured with two numbers:
systolic (top, during heartbeat) over diastolic (bottom, at rest between beats).

Stages (per ACC/AHA 2017 guidelines)
- Normal: < 120/80 mmHg
- Elevated: 120-129 / < 80 mmHg
- Stage 1: 130-139 / 80-89 mmHg
- Stage 2: >= 140 / >= 90 mmHg
- Hypertensive crisis: > 180 / > 120 mmHg (requires immediate medical care)

Common Symptoms
Most patients are asymptomatic ("silent killer"). Severe hypertension may cause
headaches, blurred vision, chest pain, shortness of breath, or nosebleeds.

Risk Factors
- Age > 60
- Family history of hypertension or cardiovascular disease
- High sodium diet, low potassium intake
- Obesity and physical inactivity
- Excessive alcohol consumption
- Chronic stress
- Diabetes and chronic kidney disease

First-Line Treatment
Lifestyle changes: DASH diet, sodium restriction (< 2300 mg/day, ideally 1500),
regular aerobic exercise (150 min/week), weight loss, limited alcohol.

Pharmacologic options (start when BP >= 140/90 or >= 130/80 with high ASCVD risk):
- Thiazide diuretics (e.g., hydrochlorothiazide 12.5-25 mg daily)
- ACE inhibitors (e.g., lisinopril 10-40 mg daily)
- ARBs (e.g., losartan 50-100 mg daily)
- Calcium channel blockers (e.g., amlodipine 5-10 mg daily)

Monitoring
Home BP monitoring twice daily is recommended for newly diagnosed patients.
Follow-up every 3-6 months once stable. Annual labs: creatinine, potassium, lipid panel.

When to Seek Emergency Care
BP > 180/120 with chest pain, vision changes, weakness, difficulty speaking,
or severe headache -> call emergency services immediately.
